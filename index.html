Ethical Analysis Examples

Ethical decision-making is central to professional interpreting. Below are examples demonstrating my approach to navigating complex ethical scenarios in accordance with the RID Code of Professional Conduct.

Case Study 1: VRS Call with a Scam Caller

Situation

You are a VRS interpreter and your caller gets a scam call.

Possible Approaches

  1. Hint that it's a scam call.
  2. Tell them it's a scam call.
  3. Transfer the call.

Interpreter’s Decision

Hint that it's a scam call so the consumer has all the information they need to recognize the situation themselves.

Supporting CPC Tenets

  • 2.2 — Assess consumer needs and ensure they have every detail required to figure it out.
  • 2.3 — Render the message faithfully.
  • 4.4 — The consumer has the right to full access the same as a hearing person would.

Short-Term Benefits

  • Signer: Might still get scammed, but retains autonomy.
  • Non-Signer: Continues their attempted call.
  • Interpreter: Stays within the role as interpreter.
  • Interpreting Community: Trusted.

Long-Term Benefits

  • Signer: Feels respected.
  • Non-Signer: Continues their business.
  • Interpreter: Interprets as normal.
  • Interpreting Community: Remains trusted.

There would never be considerations where I would not follow the CPCs.

Case Study 2: Signer Asks the Interpreter for Advice

Situation

During a VRS call, the signer asks the interpreter what they should do.

Possible Approaches

  1. Tell them what you think they should do.
  2. Let them know they can decide.
  3. Transfer the call to another interpreter.

Interpreter’s Decision

Tell the consumer they can decide for themselves.

Supporting CPC Tenets

  • 2.5 — Refrain from providing counsel, advice, or personal opinions.
  • 2.6 — Judiciously provide information or referral regarding available interpreting or community resources without infringing upon consumers’ rights. Let them make their own decisions.
  • 3.3 — Do not act as the consumer’s support person.

Short-Term Benefits

  • Signer: Makes the decision themselves.
  • Non-Signer: Not involved.
  • Interpreter: Does not get caught in the situation.
  • Interpreting Community: All are held to the same standard of service.

Long-Term Benefits

  • Signer: Understands that the interpreter is not there to give counsel.
  • Non-Signer: Not involved.
  • Interpreter: Stays within the role of interpreting only.
  • Interpreting Community: Shares the same expectations across all interpreters.

There is no reason not to follow the CPCs.
